## Summary

The NHS waiting list reached a record 7.6 million patients in 2024. This proposal commits £40bn over 5 years to restore NHS capacity: recruit 50,000 nurses and 10,000 GPs, eliminate the backlog by 2030, and fund mental health to reach 10% of NHS spending (currently 7%).

## The NHS crisis in numbers

The NHS (National Health Service) provides free universal healthcare to 67 million people — the largest single-payer system in the world by coverage. But it is in crisis:

| Indicator | 2010 | 2019 | 2024 |
|-----------|------|------|------|
| NHS spending (% GDP) | 8.5% | 7.8% | 8.2% |
| Waiting list | 2.4M | 4.4M | **7.6M** |
| Ambulance response (critical) | 8 min | 11 min | **28 min** |
| Mental health waiting (CAMHS) | 4 weeks | 8 weeks | **18 weeks** |
| GPs per 100,000 | 65 | 60 | **54** |

The 2010–2019 austerity decade reduced NHS real-terms spending growth to zero while demand grew 2–3%/year — creating a structural deficit of capacity.

## Proposal

**5-year restoration plan:**

1. **Workforce:** Recruit 50,000 nurses + 10,000 GPs + 10,000 mental health practitioners
   - Increase nursing training places from 25,000 to 40,000/year
   - Restore the nursing bursary (abolished 2017) at £12,000/year
   - International recruitment: expand visas for healthcare workers in shortage

2. **Waiting list:** Achieve 18-week maximum wait target (legal since 2004, routinely breached since 2012)
   - Expand weekend and evening services
   - Community diagnostic hubs: 100 new centres

3. **Mental health:** Increase mental health share of NHS budget from 7% to 10% by 2030
   - Parity of esteem: no patient waits >4 weeks for mental health assessment
   - 1,000 new talking therapy (IAPT) teams

4. **Prevention:** Invest 1% of NHS budget in prevention (currently 0.5%) — vaccinations, screening, public health

**Funding:** £40bn over 5 years = £8bn/year additional NHS spending.
Sources: NHS workforce levy on private providers + corporation tax restoration to 26% + NHS productivity savings.

## Evidence base

Commonwealth Fund (Mirror Mirror 2021): NHS ranks #4 overall, but #10 on healthcare outcomes — below its 2017 ranking. The gap is not structural (single-payer works) but resource-driven.

Papanicolas (JAMA 2018): UK spends £3,800/capita on healthcare vs £5,400 in France — 30% below comparable systems. Closing half this gap would eliminate the waiting list and workforce shortage.

## Arguments for

- Single-payer NHS is the most efficient model for universal coverage (lowest admin overhead: 12% vs 34% in US)
- Waiting list elimination has direct economic benefits: £7bn/year in lost productivity from people unable to work while waiting
- Broad public support: 87% of Britons value the NHS "one of the most important things about being British" (Ipsos 2024)
- Mental health parity reduces long-term economic burden (mental illness costs UK £118bn/year)

## Arguments against

- £8bn/year requires new taxes or deficit spending during fiscal consolidation
- Workforce cannot be recruited instantly — 3–5 year pipeline for nurses and GPs
- NHS productivity gap: spending more without reform may not reduce waiting lists if workflow inefficiencies persist
- Private sector growth (BUPA, Spire) may draw staff away from NHS regardless of pay increases

## Estimated impact

- Waiting list: 7.6M → <2M by 2030
- Ambulance response time: restored to 8-minute target
- GP access: <2 weeks wait for appointment (currently 3–5 weeks in most areas)
- Mental health waits: <4 weeks CAMHS (currently 18 weeks)
