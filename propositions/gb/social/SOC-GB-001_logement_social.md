## Summary

England faces its worst housing crisis since the 1960s: 1.3 million households on social housing waiting lists, 112,000 families in temporary accommodation, and average house prices 9× median earnings in London. This proposal commits to building 300,000 genuinely affordable homes per year — with 90,000 specifically social rent.

## The UK housing crisis

| Indicator | 1980 | 2000 | 2024 |
|-----------|------|------|------|
| Social homes built per year | 100,000+ | 20,000 | **8,000** |
| Social housing waiting list | 1.0M | 1.0M | **1.3M** |
| House price / earnings ratio (London) | 4× | 6× | **9×** |
| Households in temporary accommodation | — | 50,000 | **112,000** |
| Renters as share of households | 11% | 28% | **37%** |

The core problem: the Right to Buy policy (Thatcher, 1980) sold 2 million social homes at discount and replacement has been minimal ever since. The private rented sector has grown to fill the gap — but at market prices unaffordable to low-income households.

## Proposal

**300,000 homes/year target (England), of which:**
- 90,000 social rent (regulated at 50–60% of market rent)
- 60,000 affordable rent (80% of market rent)
- 150,000 market rate (through planning requirements on private developers)

**Mechanisms:**
1. **Land reform:** Compulsory purchase of developable land at existing use value (not speculative future value) — saves £50bn in land cost per million homes built
2. **Council housing direct delivery:** Restore local authority borrowing powers to build directly (suspended 2010–2018, partially restored but insufficient)
3. **Planning reform:** Mandatory 40% affordable housing requirement on all major private developments
4. **Right to Buy reform:** Freeze discounts and require 1-for-1 replacement of every sold social home in same borough
5. **Housing First integration:** 10,000 Housing First units within the 90,000 social rent target (see SOC-FR-002 model)

## Evidence base

England's housing crisis is one of the most studied globally. The consensus: undersupply is the primary driver of unaffordability, and social housing provision is the most cost-effective intervention for the lowest income quintile.

NBER research (housing affordability) consistently finds: every 1% increase in housing supply reduces rents by 0.5–1% in the same market, with spillover effects on adjacent markets. The UK has the lowest housing construction rate in Western Europe (4 homes per 1,000 inhabitants vs 6–8 in Germany, France, and Sweden).

## Arguments for

- 1.3M households waiting — the demand is demonstrated and quantified
- Social housing is more cost-effective than housing benefit (£18bn/year currently paid to private landlords)
- Land value reform could finance most of the programme (capture £50bn+ in planning gain)
- Long-term asset for the public: unlike housing benefit, social homes remain public assets

## Arguments against

- Planning system is slow — 300,000/year requires fundamental reform of local authority capacity
- Construction workforce shortage: 700,000 additional construction workers needed
- Land value reform faces legal challenges and political resistance from landowners
- Spatial mismatch: housing needed in expensive cities, but cheaper to build in periphery

## Estimated impact

- Waiting list: 1.3M → <400K by 2035
- Temporary accommodation families: 112,000 → <20,000
- Rent-to-income ratio (bottom quintile): from 45% → 30% in 15 years
- 300,000 construction jobs created
