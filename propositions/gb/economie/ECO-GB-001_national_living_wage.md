## Summary

Raise the UK National Living Wage from £11.44/hour (2024) to £15/hour by 2027, then index it to two-thirds of median earnings — bringing the UK in line with the most ambitious minimum wage standards in Europe.

## Context

The UK introduced the National Minimum Wage in 1999 and renamed it National Living Wage (NLW) in 2016 — though it remains below the Living Wage Foundation's true living wage calculation (£13.15/hour in 2024).

| Benchmark | 2024 |
|-----------|------|
| National Living Wage (legal) | £11.44/hour |
| Living Wage Foundation (London) | £13.85/hour |
| Living Wage Foundation (rest of UK) | £13.15/hour |
| Proposal (2027) | **£15.00/hour** |
| France SMIC (equivalent) | ~£10.80/hour |

## Proposal

1. **Phase-in:** £12.50 (2025) → £13.50 (2026) → **£15.00 (2027)**
2. **Youth rates abolished:** same rate for all workers ≥18 (current UK law has lower rates for under-23s)
3. **Indexation:** once at £15, indexed to 2/3 of median earnings (currently ~£17/hour median → floor = ~£11.33 — our target raises the floor above current median practice)
4. **Enforcement:** quadruple HMRC enforcement teams (currently 800 inspectors for 34 million workers)

## Evidence base

Cengiz et al. (NBER 2024) — 72-study meta-analysis: median employment elasticity of −0.13. Only 13 cents of every £1 gain from minimum wage increases is offset by employment losses. The UK Low Pay Commission's own evidence: the NLW increases since 2016 have not caused measurable job losses.

The UK experience 2016–2024 (from £6.50 to £11.44 = +76%) with zero measurable unemployment increase provides the strongest natural experiment evidence globally for the resilience of labour markets to minimum wage increases.

## Arguments for

- £11.44/hour is insufficient to cover basic living costs in most UK cities (ONS household expenditure data)
- The UK track record of 8 years of large increases without employment harm is the strongest evidence available globally
- Reduces in-work poverty (3.8 million workers in poverty despite being employed, JRF 2024)
- Reduces public spending on in-work benefits (Housing Benefit, Universal Credit)

## Arguments against

- Hospitality, care, and retail sectors employ many low-wage workers — margin pressure is real
- Rural areas and small employers face higher relative cost increases
- £15/hour approaches median wage — risk of wage compression in mid-skill occupations
- Abolishing youth rates may reduce employer willingness to hire under-23s as first job

## Estimated impact

- Workers receiving direct pay increase: ~4.5 million (workers below £15/hour)
- Median affected worker gain: +£4,200/year
- Public benefit savings: −£2–3bn/year (reduced in-work benefit claims)
- Employment effect: −0.5 to −1.5% for directly affected workers (consistent with meta-analysis)
