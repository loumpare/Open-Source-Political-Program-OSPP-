## Summary

Japan ranks 118th globally on gender equality (WEF 2024), with a 21% gender pay gap and only 13% of corporate board seats held by women. Womenomics 3.0 proposes mandatory pay transparency, universal childcare, and equal parental leave to close the gap by 2035.

## Japan's gender inequality in numbers

| Indicator | Japan | OECD avg | Top (Iceland) |
|-----------|-------|----------|--------------|
| Gender pay gap | 21.3% | 11.9% | 4.1% |
| Female labour force participation | 53% | 65% | 80% |
| Corporate board seats (women) | 13% | 28% | 44% |
| Mothers returning to work after first child | 35% | 65% | 80% |
| Parental leave taken by fathers | 14% | 42% | 90% (IS) |

Japan's low female participation costs the economy an estimated ¥40 trillion (2.5% GDP) in unrealised productive capacity (McKinsey Global Institute 2015 — still current per IMF 2023 update).

## Proposal: Womenomics 3.0

**1. Gender pay transparency (2025):**
- Companies with >100 employees must publish annual gender pay gap report (methodology: same as UK Gender Pay Gap Reporting since 2017)
- Companies with gap >10% must submit a closure plan within 1 year
- Public procurement exclusion for non-compliant firms >500 employees after 2027

**2. Universal childcare (2026–2030):**
- Eliminate the 380,000-child waiting list for licensed childcare (hoikuen)
- Build 500,000 new licensed childcare places by 2028
- Cap cost at ¥5,000/month (currently ¥30,000–¥60,000/month in Tokyo)

**3. Equal parental leave (2025):**
- Make fathers' parental leave non-optional for 4 weeks (current take-up: 14% despite legal right)
- Replace with 12 weeks minimum for each parent, non-transferable
- Employer penalty for discouraging leave: ¥10M ($70,000)

**4. Corporate board quotas (2027):**
- Require 30% women on boards of listed companies by 2027 (France achieved 44% via Copé-Zimmermann law 2011→2017)

## Evidence base

Goldin (Nobel 2023): the "child penalty" explains most of Japan's gender pay gap. Japanese mothers who exit the workforce permanently after first birth account for 65% of the total female/male earnings gap. Childcare availability is the single largest predictor of maternal return to work (IZA research across 15 OECD countries).

Denmark's experience: closing the childcare gap raised female labour force participation by 7.5pp in 5 years (same finding as Quebec childcare programme, SOC-CA-001).

## Arguments for

- Economic necessity: Japan's aging population requires every adult in the workforce
- Pay transparency works: UK firms required to report since 2017 → gap narrowed 3pp in 6 years
- Board quotas work: France's Copé-Zimmermann law took boards from 10% to 44% in 7 years
- Childcare ROI: same as Quebec model — near self-financing through tax revenues from returned mothers

## Arguments against

- Cultural resistance: "M-curve" employment pattern deeply embedded in social norms
- Corporate resistance: mandatory quotas opposed by Keidanren
- Childcare workforce shortage (hoikuen teachers severely underpaid and understaffed)
- Pay transparency may trigger wage compression at top levels rather than raising floors

## Estimated impact

- Gender pay gap: 21.3% → 13% by 2035 (based on French trajectory)
- Female labour force participation: 53% → 65% by 2030 (OECD average)
- GDP gain from participation increase: +2 to +3% (McKinsey/IMF modelling)
- Childcare waiting list: 380,000 → <50,000 by 2028
