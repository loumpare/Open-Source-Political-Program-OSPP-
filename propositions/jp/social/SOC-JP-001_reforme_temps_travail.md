## Summary

Japan's 2018 Work Style Reform Law capped overtime at 100 hours/month — still more than double the EU maximum (48h/week total). This proposal reduces the cap to 45 hours/month, enforces it with criminal penalties, and creates a national karoshi (death from overwork) prevention programme.

## The karoshi crisis

**Karoshi** (過労死) — death from overwork — is a documented medical and legal phenomenon in Japan:
- 10,000+ karoshi deaths recognised annually (Ministry of Labour, 2023)
- Real estimate (including unreported): 30,000–50,000/year (Nippon Medical Journal 2021)
- Suicide from work-related stress: 2,100 cases recognized in 2022

**Root cause: extreme working hours**
- Japan: average 1,747 hours worked/year (OECD 2023)
- Germany: 1,349 hours/year
- Denmark: 1,570 hours/year
- Salaried workers in Japan: 22% work >60 hours/week

**The 2018 reform was insufficient:**
- Cap set at 100h overtime/month (=60h total/week) — still double EU Working Time Directive (48h)
- Enforcement: administrative fines only, no criminal liability
- Exemptions: doctors, researchers, construction until 2024

## Proposal

1. **Overtime cap:** Reduce from 100h to **45h per month maximum** (= 56h/week total — still above EU but achievable in 5 years)
2. **Criminal enforcement:** Prison term (up to 1 year) for company directors who knowingly exceed the cap — equivalent to French "mise en danger d'autrui"
3. **Right to disconnect:** Legal right to not respond to professional communications outside working hours (modelled on French loi Travail 2016)
4. **Karoshi recognition:** Automatic compensation for overtime-related illness/death above 80h/month (reduce current threshold from 80h to 60h)
5. **4-day optional week:** Government pilot with 100 public sector organisations — 4 days/week with full pay (modelled on Iceland 2015–2019)

## Evidence base

Lepinteur (IZA 2020): reductions in working hours improve worker wellbeing significantly with no measurable productivity loss when accompanied by work organisation changes. Japan's own experience: the voluntary 4-day week at Panasonic (2022) → productivity +40%, turnover −35%.

OECD data: Japan has 25% lower GDP/hour worked than Germany, despite working 30% more hours — suggesting working less would not reduce economic output.

## Arguments for

- Karoshi is a public health emergency — documented, quantified, preventable
- Japan's demographic crisis (lowest birth rate in OECD) is partly driven by overwork discouraging family formation
- Productivity per hour would increase (Lepinteur, Japan government pilots)
- Right to disconnect already legally established in France (2016) and Spain (2018) with no economic harm

## Arguments against

- Culture of presenteeism (face time over results) deeply embedded in Japanese workplace culture
- SME compliance difficult: 70% of karoshi cases involve firms <100 employees
- Resistance from Keidanren (major employers' federation)
- Enforcement capacity: Labour Standards Inspection Offices are severely understaffed

## Estimated impact

- Karoshi deaths: −30 to −50% if 45h overtime cap strictly enforced
- Productivity/hour: +15% over 10 years (consistent with cross-country data)
- Birth rate: +0.1–0.2 children/woman (OECD correlation between working hours and fertility)
- Absenteeism: −20% (burnout reduction)
