## Summary

By 2030, 30% of Japan's population will be over 65 — the highest ratio in history. This proposal reforms Japan's Long-Term Care Insurance system (LTCI) to guarantee dignity, delay institutionalisation, and support the 6 million family caregivers who sacrifice their careers.

## Japan's demographic reality

Japan is the world's "oldest" society:
- 29% of population >65 in 2024 → projected 38% by 2050
- 6 million people provide informal (unpaid) care to elderly relatives
- 100,000 people per year leave employment to become caregivers ("kaigo rishoku")
- Average cost of institutional care: ¥3–5M/year (€20,000–33,000) — unaffordable for most

**The Long-Term Care Insurance (LTCI)** was introduced in 2000 and covers professional care for those assessed as needing it. But:
- 50% of care costs remain out-of-pocket for families
- Home care is underfunded vs institutional care (perverse incentive for hospitalisation)
- Caregiver workforce: 1.6 million needed by 2025 vs 1.2 million available today

## Proposal

**1. "Home First" mandate:**
- Default care pathway: home-based care, not institutionalisation
- Fund 30,000 new community care hubs (chiiki houkatsu centre) providing medical + social services locally
- 50% subsidy for home modification (ramps, bathroom aids) for all LTCI-eligible households

**2. Caregiver support:**
- Paid caregiver leave: 12 weeks/year at 80% salary for working family caregivers (currently 5 days unpaid)
- Establish 1,000 "Caregiver Respite" centres providing temporary care relief (1–4 weeks)
- Tax credit for informal caregivers: ¥500,000/year (current: ¥0)

**3. Workforce:**
- Raise caregiver wages by ¥70,000/month (current: ¥240,000 average — lowest of any professional sector)
- Accreditation of foreign care workers (streamlined visa for care worker category)
- 500,000 new care worker training programmes by 2027

**4. Technology:**
- National programme for care robotics deployment (Japan is global leader — Panasonic HAL, CYBERDYNE)
- ¥1 trillion (€7 billion) 5-year fund for care technology R&D and deployment

## Evidence base

International evidence consistently shows that community-based care (Home First) is:
- More cost-effective than institutional care (30–50% less expensive per patient)
- Better for quality of life and longevity (WHO systematic review 2022)
- Preferred by 85% of elderly patients in OECD surveys

Japan's own LTCI data: patients receiving home care show 40% lower hospitalisation rates than equivalent institutional care patients (MHLW 2023).

## Arguments for

- Existential demographic challenge — inaction is not an option
- Home care is cheaper, better, and what patients prefer
- Raising caregiver wages reduces the 400,000 annual turnover (80% annual rate in care sector)
- Technology deployment plays to Japan's industrial strengths

## Arguments contre

- Scale of need is unprecedented globally — no country has managed 30% over-65 successfully
- Immigration required for workforce — politically sensitive in Japan
- Care technology cannot replace human connection and complex care needs
- Cost: estimated ¥8–10 trillion/year additional (6–8% of current government spending)

## Estimated impact

- "Kaigo rishoku" (career exit for care): −40% (from 100,000 → 60,000/year)
- Institutional care rate: from 20% of LTCI patients to 12% by 2035
- Care worker wages: ¥240,000 → ¥310,000/month (competitive with median worker)
- Caregiver burnout: −30% (respite centres + paid leave)
