## Summary

Create "Medicare Part E" — a public health insurance option open to any individual or employer — with automatic free enrollment for the uninsured below 200% FPL, and expand Medicare drug price negotiation to the full formulary.

## Proposal

~26 million Americans remain uninsured (2024), and ~45 million are underinsured (spending >10% of income on healthcare). An estimated 68,000 deaths per year are attributable to lack of insurance.

**Medicare Part E — public option:**
- Open enrollment for any US resident not covered by employer insurance or Medicaid/Medicare
- Premiums set at actual cost (no profit margin), with subsidies for households <400% FPL
- **Automatic enrollment at $0 premium** for uninsured adults <200% FPL
- Benefit design: matches current Medicare (Parts A+B) plus dental, vision, mental health (parity)
- Provider reimbursement: Medicare rates +15% for primary care to incentivise participation

**Drug price negotiation:**
- Extend Medicare's negotiation authority (IRA 2022, currently 10 drugs) to full Medicare formulary
- Price reference: OECD median (US drugs cost 2.78× OECD average, RAND 2021)
- Estimated savings: $120–200B/year on drug spending

**Financing:**
- Premiums from enrolled members
- Federal subsidy for automatic enrollment: ~$80B/year
- Drug savings fund additional coverage

## Evidence base

- **Administrative overhead (Himmelstein et al., Lancet 2020):** US healthcare administrative costs = 34.2% of total spending ($812B) vs. 12.4% in Canada. A public option reduces this overhead for enrolled members by ~50%.
- **International comparison (Papanicolas et al., JAMA 2018):** US spends $11,100/capita vs. $5,500–6,700 in peer countries — for worse outcomes on 10 of 11 quality metrics.
- **Medicaid and mortality (Finkelstein et al., NBER 2019):** Medicaid expansion reduces adult mortality by ~6% for newly covered populations.
- **Uninsurance and mortality (Woolhandler & Himmelstein, Lancet 2020):** 68,000 deaths per year in the US are attributable to lack of health insurance.
- **Drug prices (RAND 2021):** US pays 2.78× OECD average for the same branded drugs. Negotiation to OECD median saves $120–200B/year.

## Arguments for

- Covers 26M+ uninsured without mandating employer coverage changes
- Market competition may reduce private insurance premiums
- Drug price negotiation delivers immediate savings regardless of take-up
- Broad public support: 63% of Americans support a public option (Gallup 2024)
- States with Medicaid expansion already show measurable mortality reductions

## Arguments against

- Risk of adverse selection (sicker patients enroll; healthier stay with employer plans)
- Provider resistance to Medicare reimbursement rates (some providers refuse Medicare patients)
- Political opposition from private insurance industry ($1T market at risk)
- $80B/year federal subsidy requires new revenue or deficit financing
- Does not address underlying healthcare cost drivers (fee-for-service model)

## Estimated impact

- Newly covered: ~26 million uninsured + significant reduction in underinsurance
- Deaths prevented: ~50,000–68,000/year
- Drug savings: $120–200B/year
- Net federal cost: ~$200–300B/year (gross $450B, offset by premiums + savings)
