## Summary

Enact a federal carbon price starting at $65/tCO₂ in 2027, rising by $15/year, with 100% of revenue returned as equal per-capita quarterly dividends to all US residents.

## Proposal

The US has no federal carbon price. The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) uses incentives but not pricing — insufficient to reach 2030 targets. This proposal establishes an upstream carbon fee:

**Fee schedule:**
| Year | $/tCO₂ | Annual dividend/adult |
|------|--------|----------------------|
| 2027 | 65 | ~$600 |
| 2030 | 110 | ~$900 |
| 2033 | 155 | ~$1,200 |
| 2037 | 200 | ~$1,500 |

**Point of application:** upstream — at the wellhead, mine mouth, or port of entry. No paperwork for businesses or consumers.

**Dividend:** 100% of revenue distributed quarterly as equal per-capita checks to all US legal residents. Children receive a half-share (paid to parents/guardians).

**Border carbon adjustment (CBAM):** imports from countries without equivalent carbon pricing face a border levy, protecting US competitiveness and incentivising global adoption.

## Evidence base

- **Social Cost of Carbon (Rennert et al., Nature 2022):** $185/tCO₂ — the proposed schedule reaches this level by ~2034, aligning economic incentives with full climate damages.
- **Distributional effects (Goulder et al., NBER 2019):** when revenue is recycled as equal dividends, the bottom 60% of households come out net positive.
- **Emissions reductions:** Columbia SIPA/REMI modelling of the EICDA: −45% US GHG emissions vs 2005 levels by 2035.
- **Jobs:** net +2.1 million jobs in clean energy by 2035 (REMI 2023).

## Arguments for

- Most cost-efficient mechanism for emission reductions (broad coverage, price discovery)
- Revenue neutrality: no government spending increase
- Progressive: dividends benefit lower-income households disproportionately
- Bipartisan history: supported by economists across political spectrum (George Shultz, Janet Yellen, ExxonMobil, etc.)
- Border adjustment protects US industry and exports policy globally

## Arguments against

- Political resistance from fossil fuel states (West Virginia, Wyoming, Texas)
- $65/ton is below current estimates of social cost of carbon (~$185/ton)
- Phase-in may be too slow for urgent 2030 Paris targets
- Dividend may be politically framed as a "tax rebate" rather than wealth redistribution

## Estimated impact

- GHG reduction: −45% by 2035 vs 2005
- Dividend recipients: ~260 million adult residents
- Bottom 60% households: net positive financially
- Revenue: $300–500B/year
