## Summary

Establish a federal paid family and medical leave programme guaranteeing 12 weeks of paid leave at 90% wage replacement for all US workers, funded via a small shared payroll contribution.

## Proposal

The US is the only OECD country without federal paid family or medical leave. Approximately 73% of US workers have no employer-provided paid family leave, and only 21% have any access to paid medical leave.

**Programme structure:**
- **Eligible events:** new child (birth, adoption, foster placement), serious personal illness (non-work-related), or caring for a seriously ill immediate family member
- **Duration:** 12 weeks per year per eligible event
- **Wage replacement:** 90% for workers earning ≤ median wage ($1,100/week); 66% for wages above median (capped at $1,650/week)
- **Eligibility:** all workers with ≥ 3 months employment — including part-time, gig, and self-employed workers

**Financing:**
- 0.2% payroll tax, split equally between employee (0.1%) and employer (0.1%)
- Administered by the Social Security Administration
- Maximum annual contribution per worker: ~$280

**Implementation:** modelled on California (2004), New Jersey (2009), New York (2018), and Washington (2020) state programmes, which have demonstrated feasibility and positive outcomes.

## Evidence base

- **California PFL (2004+), Rossin-Slater et al. (NBER):** California's paid family leave increased maternal employment by +10 percentage points at 1 year postpartum; infant breastfeeding duration increased significantly.
- **Goldin (Nobel 2023):** Paid leave, especially when fathers take equal leave, reduces the "child penalty" on women's careers — the single largest driver of the gender pay gap.
- **IZA DP13990 (Cools et al.):** Norwegian paid leave studies show no negative employment effects for employers; reduced worker turnover saves firms ~$3,000–5,000 per covered worker.
- **Business impact (California experience):** 91% of California employers report no negative business impact; 49% report improved morale and productivity.

## Arguments for

- Only OECD country without this protection — US workers at clear disadvantage vs peers
- Strong evidence of positive health outcomes for parents and children
- Reduces gender pay gap when fathers take leave
- Minimal cost: ~$280/year per worker maximum
- State programmes demonstrate feasibility and popularity

## Arguments against

- Political resistance: framed as a "job-killing mandate" by some employer groups
- 12-week maximum is below OECD median (18 weeks average for maternity leave)
- Gig economy classification challenges — not all workers covered under standard payroll definitions
- Some small employers will face operational challenges replacing workers simultaneously

## Estimated impact

- Workers newly covered: ~100 million (currently without any paid leave)
- Annual programme cost: ~$20B (largely offset by 0.2% payroll contribution)
- Reduction in infant mortality: estimated −5–8% for low-income births (based on California data)
- Gender pay gap reduction: −3 to −5 pp over 10 years (if fathers take leave at Norwegian rates)
