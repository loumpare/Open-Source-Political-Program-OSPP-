## Summary

Establish federally funded, voluntary universal pre-kindergarten for all 3- and 4-year-olds in the United States, with binding quality standards and pay parity for educators.

## Proposal

Only 44% of US 4-year-olds and 18% of 3-year-olds are enrolled in public preschool. Access is sharply stratified by income: children from the top income quintile are 3× more likely to attend high-quality preschool than those from the bottom quintile.

**Federal-state structure:**
- Federal block grants cover 60% of program cost; states match 40%
- States must meet federal quality standards to receive funding
- Voluntary for families; automatic enrollment for income-eligible households (Medicaid/SNAP eligible)

**Quality standards:**
- Class size ≤ 15 children with 1 lead teacher + 1 aide
- Lead teachers must hold a bachelor's degree in early childhood education or equivalent
- Pay parity with K–12 certified teachers (currently pre-K median wage is 40% lower)
- Evidence-based curriculum with structured play, language-rich environments, and social-emotional learning

**Target:** Universal access by 2031 (6-year phase-in), building on and expanding Head Start for income-eligible families.

## Evidence base

- **Perry Preschool RCT (Michigan, 40-year follow-up, Heckman):** ROI of $7–12 per $1 invested. Participants have −57% crime rates, +22% earnings in adulthood.
- **Abecedarian Project:** Full-day early education from birth to age 5 → IQ gains of 4–5 points, special education referrals halved, higher college graduation rates.
- **Boston Pre-K (Chetty lab):** Universal preschool access → +8pp college enrollment, measurable reduction in incarceration.
- **OECD (2025):** US spends 0.3% GDP on early childhood education vs. 0.6–0.8% OECD average.

## Arguments for

- Closes the readiness gap between high- and low-income children before kindergarten
- High ROI through long-term earnings, reduced crime, and lower special education costs
- Supports parental (especially maternal) labor force participation
- Bipartisan support historically: Head Start was created under LBJ, expanded under Republican administrations

## Arguments against

- High upfront federal cost (~$120B/year)
- Quality varies widely in existing state programs — funding alone does not guarantee quality
- Teacher pipeline shortage: not enough certified early childhood educators to staff universal programs immediately
- Debates over appropriate pedagogy (structured vs. play-based)

## Estimated impact

- Children reached: ~6.6 million aged 3–4
- Federal cost: ~$120B/year (phased)
- Long-run fiscal ROI: $7–12 per $1 over 30 years (Heckman estimates)
